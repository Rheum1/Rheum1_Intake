Fibromyalgia

Understanding chronic pain and how to manage it

Overview

Fibromyalgia is a chronic condition characterized by widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, sleep disturbances, and cognitive difficulties. It affects how the brain and spinal cord process pain signals, amplifying painful sensations. The cause is unclear, but it often coexists with conditions like IBS, migraines, or autoimmune diseases.

Common Symptoms

  • Widespread muscle pain (lasting 3+ months)
  • Chronic fatigue
  • “Fibro fog” – memory and focus issues
  • Non-restorative sleep or insomnia
  • Depression or anxiety
  • Headaches or migraines
  • Irritable bowel or bladder
  • Heightened sensitivity to touch, sound, or temperature

Diagnosis

There is no single test for fibromyalgia. Diagnosis relies on your medical history, a physical exam, and ruling out other conditions like thyroid issues or autoimmune diseases. Blood tests may be done to exclude other causes.

Treatment Options

  • Medications: Pain relievers, antidepressants (e.g., duloxetine), anti-seizure meds (e.g., pregabalin)
  • Therapies: Physical therapy, cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), sleep hygiene strategies
  • Lifestyle: Regular gentle exercise, stress management (yoga, mindfulness)

Living With Fibromyalgia

  • Practice pacing—don’t overdo it on “good” days
  • Keep moving with gentle, regular exercise
  • Track symptoms and identify triggers
  • Explore dietary changes that may reduce inflammation
  • Join support groups for encouragement and ideas

FAQs

Is fibromyalgia an autoimmune disease?

No, but it often coexists with autoimmune disorders.

Can men get fibromyalgia?

Yes. Though more common in women, men can also be affected.

Will it get worse over time?

Symptoms can vary, but fibromyalgia doesn’t usually cause tissue or joint damage.
